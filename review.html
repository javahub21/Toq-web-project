1.5 - Describe copyright and constraints affecting websites


Copyright refers to a legal protection of ones original work as the author of that content. Content you have made live online is easy for certain parties to steal and claim as their own, on the other hand websites with written material, graphics, business logo are firmly categorised as copyright material. UK Logo registered trademark and copyright, designs and patents Act 1988 protects creations of literary work, music, art, sound and film which limits what you can add to your website unless you have legal rights for paid content e.g. music, film, art…etc [1]


1.6 - Describing access issues that need taking into account


Accessibility tools such as keyboard only, touch screen and voice recognition are designed to provide people with a disability to access/use a website e.g. speech, visual, auditory..etc People with a disability face vision loss, deaf/hard hearing, colour blindness e.g. poor use of colour contrast on text, background, nav bars, buttons. Adding in shortkeys (within HTML to navigate to links), inserting names for images in the Alt tag in HTML, using text transcripts to make audio information accessible. You can also test your website code for accessibility on https://webaccessibility.com/ so it’s accessible to disabled people [2]


1.7 - Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for website


These are the file format types I used to make sure my website would run in different web browsers (Google Chrome, Firefox, Safari), all files named in lowercase as well e.g. .html - Hyper Text Markup Language (defines headings, paragraphs, hyperlinks, lists), .css - Cascading Style sheets (layout structure of web page, text/image/nav bar positioning, < link > tag is used in HTML to link .css file), .jpg/.jpeg - Joint Photographic Experts Group (store saved compressed images to use on your website). These file types are used to create a business or personal website that are made public in a web browser.
